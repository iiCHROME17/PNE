# It's Time We Kill The UK's Misery Culture

## Pessimism

The UK is a pessimistic place, especially for creatives. It’s not just unfriendly, it's structured in a way that makes it far too easy to become a target rather than a pioneer. We’re all accustomed to the idea that the social climate is bad vibes, honestly it makes sense.

Everyone who wants to try. Everyone who wants to experiment. They’re scared. Self-expression here isn’t revered; it’s consumed for the pleasure of people who wouldn’t dare to try themselves.

So if the running culture raises the barriers to entry for creative minds, what becomes the practical defence?

It’s not tougher skin.

Take your mind back to a playground. Does a bully target someone in a group? No. They target whoever stands alone. The logic really is no different from today.

I believe the best way to lower the barriers to entry for a creative is specifically micro-communities. Small and deliberately built hubs that protect experimental minds long enough for them to provide something to the culture.

## Community Creates Immunity

Mockery is something everyone fears. There are people out there who want to try something, who have something genuinely worth making, and they won’t because they’re scared of becoming a target. It’s a rational fear, experimenting is a vulnerable process. You are acting with more intuition than guidance, which means you’re exposed in a way that might feel impossible to defend.

Bullying & mockery distorts the process. It introduces a useless noise when what you need most is signal. Instead of generative feedback about what works and what isn’t, you get the ego of people who wouldn’t even try. That noise is what kills experiments before they become anything.

The defence isn’t tougher skin. It’s to belong somewhere.

Not a massive collective. Not a movement with a name and a brand. Just a reliably passionate group of people who care as much as you do. The primary function of that group isn’t to make you feel cooler or more niche than everyone else. It’s generative feedback. People who can tell you honestly which experiments work and why. People whose opinions carry actual weight because they share the process of experimenting with you.

The more genuine feedback you receive the less the noise of mockery will touch you. You will already have what you need. Feedback.

Beyond that, the risk calculation for mockery shifts entirely when someone belongs to a network. It’s easy targeting a single person. It’s difficult to target someone with people behind them. Community doesn’t just support the creative, it changes the social dynamics around them.

That protection is what allows the gestational phase to exist. The period where experimenting is still illegible and fragile. A person undergoing that process is cognitively nude and a small community is that shelter.

## The Americans

I hate to bring up the Americans again but they have very good examples of this. Odd Future. A$AP Mob. The Internet. These are all functionally micro-communities and small collectives that were passion-centric, not image. Their focus was experimenting together. Creating together.

Tyler, the Creator had one of the wildest experimental phases of any artist in recent memory. He made songs and jokes that were far more uncomfortable than funny. Critics brought that up repeatedly and shamed him heavily for it. That version of Tyler no longer exists, though. He outgrew it. He is very far from his early work; that evolution wouldn’t have happened if he were alone. Critics and backlash would’ve swallowed him. Odd Future acted as both grounding and protection during the phase that needed it most. They absorbed some of the noise and allowed him to keep going.

A$AP Rocky is a similar, yet different story. He blew up quickly in 2011. It wasn’t an isolated effort, though, A$AP Mob used Tumblr and blog culture at the time to build a specific cult following around him. The hype A$AP Rocky had was a coordinated and shared effort. Rocky had a multi-discipline crew making the ground beneath him.

Two different artists, two different stories. The mechanism is the same underneath. More minds make a bigger imaginative field. The more you can imagine, the more opportunities you can make for yourself.

And if the UK lacks opportunities, maybe we must stop waiting so much for it to come. A collective mind can create realities a single one cannot.

## The Brits

The Americans have groups like Odd Future and A$AP Mob when it comes to creatives. Who does the UK have?

A few, actually. Think of groups like Osbatt and something like Beta Squad or the Sidemen. I know those seem wildly different but they still are the same in abstract. Two groups operating around creativity, one music & fashion, the other short and long form content. I give full credit to these two groups.

However, even groups like Beta Squad are mono-disciplined. Five content creators together is likely to produce a louder version of one content creator. That is to say the ceiling is the same in most instances, just bigger. You get more of what you already have than something new. You get more Youtube / TikTok / Streaming content and just that.

That is where multidisciplined groups become the lucrative argument. At least in my opinion. When different disciplines meet, the imaginative field doesn’t expand only in size but also in form. It becomes malleable.

When a rapper, a singer and a filmmaker meet, the imaginative field here becomes much more vast. More bases get touched and more realities become possible. The shared cognitive resource generates more possible experiments. You can create your own opportunities instead of waiting for them to arrive.

A content house has one output: content. A multidisciplinary and experimental collective has outputs nobody, not even the members, could predict. Short films, music, documentaries, exhibitions, campaigns.

This matters most specifically in lower-middle and working class creative environments. Individual resources are limited here. Equipment costs money. Time costs money. A photographer who can’t afford a director doesn’t need one if a filmmaker is in the group. Pooling discipline is pooling resources. The collective makes possible what the individual cannot afford alone.

Diversity of discipline is practical infrastructure that could genuinely serve the UK’s creative economy well.

## A Puzzle Piece

What I’m describing isn’t the kind of collective we’re used to seeing. It’s not a content house or a pure career and status move. It’s for people who genuinely share a disposition. People who are passionate about what they do but don’t know where to go, at least not alone. When I say we need multidisciplined experimental collectives, if I strip all that jargon I’m saying we need people unafraid to create like children to find each other.

The Odd Future vibe wasn’t manufactured. It was what happened when those specific weird kids were in the same room. Their energy and their projects were authentic expressions of the group’s values and humour. Its purpose wasn’t performative or status-seeking. It was co-exploration.

I don’t care to be a visionary or overly prescriptive here. I just believe creation must be communal if the UK really wants to get out of the logjam it’s in.

Seek out people of different disciplines. Not a hundred. Maybe five to ten. People who want to experiment. Just build something with them and let the rest unfold.

This is just the best piece I can bring to the puzzle.
