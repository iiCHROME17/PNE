# Slawn, The New UK Prototype

## The Only Thing Slawn Has Ever Said To Me

Slawn is one of the most successful artists in the UK’s recent history and now he’s going global. He sets a new benchmark for UK creatives here. He represents a newer stance towards creation. Something that resists mockery that kills things before they get the chance to even start. I know this not from his work alone, but from the one brief thing he said to me at his Yates exhibition.

“What am I saying? I’m not an adult.”

He said it seemingly confused, more to himself than to me, I had just got a picture with him. I think it’s one of the most clarifying things a UK creative has said in years. It’s probably an unconscious part of him, but it should be a conscious lesson to us.

The permission to not see yourself as an adult. I believe that single permission is what can produce a meaningful change for the UK creative.

## I’m Not An Artist, I Just Make Art

“ I’m not an artist, I just make art.”

That’s something Slawn says a lot. It’s words I urge every creative in the UK to listen to. His rejection of the artist identity is precisely what makes him such a powerful one.

Slawn cannot fail. Seriously, he can’t. He’s not subscribed to the game. If he isn’t an artist, he can’t be held to the standards and respectability politics of one. You can see that clearly; there are plenty of people in the art world that dislike him and believe he’s just evading capture. That is all just a part of the territory he holds.

The territory of someone who cannot be touched by rules he never agreed to follow.

It connects directly to what he said to me about not being an adult. Children know best just how to make things, unbound from what making those things say about them. If you refuse the label, you refuse the cage. He has no performance to maintain, only play to conduct.

It works the same way with any identity. If being attractive becomes central to who you are, being called ugly hurts. Remove it from your sense of identity and you shrug.

It’s no different with you and your art.

## The State of Play

I believe the freest adult doesn’t believe they’re an adult. They’ll see themselves as a grown child. If you think about what makes one an adult, it’s just the acceptance of shame and the adopting of a fixed identity. A child doesn’t do either. Their primary concern is play and exploration. That is exactly where their power lies.

The purest form of ambition is found in a child’s ambition. There is no ambition more honest or more fearless than that of the child who hasn’t learned fear. I’m hardly original in this stance, in fact Kanye West talks about this repeatedly in interviews and songs.

“I Believe in the children, listen to the kids bro.” - Saint Pablo.

The childlike orientation is the creative one. To be more creative and to be more childlike are synonomous. It is losing that relationship with a childlike perception that makes people believe they are small.

And so ambition should look like play and not defence.

If you paint but you need people to confirm you’re a great painter, you’ll never be one. If you sing and you need people to like your songs, you’ll never be a great singer. There’s this concept called autotelia. The virtue is in the act itself. Once you understand that, you are already great. It’s up to the world to catch up.

That’s how children see things. It’s how Slawn sees things. His existence is proof that this isn’t some naive philosophy; the UK can tolerate playfulness as ambition. Slawn is both the evidence and the prototype.

## The Armour of Play

Slawn’s mindset resists the UK Bully Culture structurally. Most attacks on a person target what they think you are insecure about or what you need validated.

They go for the parts of your identity they believe you are attached to.

But if what you do validates itself, can it really be insulted? People won’t like what you do, that’s a feature not a bug. The fact it was created is all it means to you. Slawn has literally destroyed his own artworks in interviews. It’s not some performance or provocation. It’s him showing his non-attachment to his creations. They genuinely don’t mean anything to him once they’re created.

Slawn enjoys making and not having made. I think that’s a key part of his mindset. Finished pieces don’t contain a piece of his self-worth or identity, so nothing can be used to insult him.

I understand this pretty well. I know I shouldn’t talk about me in an essay but it is my substack, after all. I equally don’t consider myself a writer, thinker or anything but a weird man with very weird interests. There’s no identity label to live up to. No cage either. I just follow whatever I find interesting the same way a kid follows whatever catches their eye.

That freedom isn’t unique or inherently selective. It’s available to anyone who wants do something for the love of just doing it.

## The Prototype, The Saviour

Slawn’s success is proof UK Culture around creativity can change. By all means someone who doesn’t take themselves seriously and is openly playful shouldn’t survive the UK’s social climate. Slawn isn’t just surviving, he’s actively thriving. As I write  this I believe he’s in America with Lil Yachty, having just made cover art for 21 Savage. He’s no longer local talent, he’s international talent.

His existence collapses the excuse that bully culture is all there is for a creative operating in the UK. Yes, his origins are in Nigeria and that may have produced different values in him, but he still made it here. The door is open. You might just have to let go of needing what you do to be validated as what you are.

What the UK needs most, and what Slawn models best, is a different relationship with the things you make. A rejection of doing things for public acceptance, in favour of childlike satisfaction. Whatever you decide to create, make sure you are doing it for you as much as possible. Reject the label and give yourself permission to play.

I’m not offering a complete solution. I think it’s a fraction of one, but puzzles are made out of pieces.
