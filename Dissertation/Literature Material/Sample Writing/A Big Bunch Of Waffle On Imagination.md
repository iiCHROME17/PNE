# A Big Bunch Of Waffle On Imagination

## The Misery Gap

Every person starts life with 100% expressive capacity. Pure potential, capable of imagining yourself in infinite directions.

Then life happens.

Family tradition takes 15%. The unspoken rules about what “people like us” do. Cultural tradition takes another 15%. The inherited scripts about how to move through the world. Being Black takes 25%. The weight of representation, navigating a world that rarely sees you clearly. Masculine take 25%. The suppression of tenderness, the performance of hardness, or feminine scripts with the constant editing for palatability, the shrinking to make others comfortable.

That’s 80% of your expressive capacity gone.

The gap between the 20% you’re left with and the 100% you could ideally achieve. This is the Misery Gap; the distance between who you are and who you’re allowed to be. The space where resentment grows, where people hurt each other, where we try to extract from others what we cannot access within ourselves.

People don’t harm because they’re evil. They harm because they’re incomplete. Something in them is caged, and they either collapse inward with coping mechanisms or lash outward to fill the gap.

## The Imagined Reality

The solution isn’t external permission. It’s imagination.

Take any role, tradition, or identity script and strip it to its bare essentials. What’s actually required? What’s just inherited performance?

What makes someone Black? Blood. Everything else, every cultural expectation, every behavioural code, every aesthetic choice is negotiable. If a tradition widens your misery gap, you can simply just remove it. You can imagine a version of any label attached to you that fits your internal reality.

This isn’t abandoning identity. It’s reclaiming it.

What makes someone a man? A professional? An artist? A good person? Strip away the performance and reconstruct from what actually matters to you. You stop living someone else’s script.

This imagined reality isn’t fantasy. It’s seeing what could be rather than only accepting what’s been handed down.

## The Return to Childlikeness

Children hold truths lightly. They want to be a firefighter one day, a scientist the next, then an astronaut. Not because they’re inconsistent or unstable. Because they’re open. They haven’t built an identity around being right.

Adulthood trains to defend answers instead of expanding understanding. We mistake certainty for wisdom. We build fortresses around our conclusions. Teams that play defence never score. Only someone who can make room for childlikeness can succeed with their emotions.

Wisdom isn’t having the right answer. It’s remaining open to better ones. Being able to change your mind without losing yourself.

## The Threat of Identity Calcification

The moment you root your identity in being a certain type of person rather than discovering what’s true, you close yourself off.

If your sense of self depends on being “a good person” or “a rational thinker” or “someone who has their life together,” then any information that threatens that image becomes an enemy. You stop learning. You start defending.

You’ve calcified.

This is how smart people stay wrong. How compassionate people become cruel. How open-minded people become dogmatic. Once your identity depends on a position, you can’t afford to be wrong. Your ego becomes the enemy of your growth.

People cling to jobs that drain them, relationships that diminish them, beliefs that contradict reality. Letting go would mean admitting they were wrong. Admitting they were wrong would mean losing their sense of self.

You are not your conclusions. You are not your track record. You are not the story you’ve been telling about yourself. Having a vivid imagination is the capacity to keep discovering, to keep imagining, to keep reconstructing yourself in response to reality.

When you forget that, you’ve chosen image over essence. You’ve chosen to be a monument instead of a living thing.

## The Integration

Kanye says: “Stop being controlled by fear and let the universe guide your actions in love.”

Take that seriously. If you program your imagination to revolve around openness over defensiveness , then change and growth no longer feel threatening. They become modes of curiosity . You don’t have to be right; you won’t even want to be right . There’s no need to defend an image of yourself that doesn’t serve you.

The goal is alignment: let your imagination guide you toward whatever reality supports your mental health, creativity, and authenticity best . Strip away inherited scripts, performative expectations, and fear-based habits. The life you inhabit doesn’t have to be a replay of someone else’s blueprint.

Openness is a practice. Curiosity is an action. Each moment you face a difficult question, each time you allow yourself to reconsider, to experiment, or to imagine differently, you shrink the misery gap . Courage becomes your tool, not conflict. Failure becomes your teacher, not your jailer.

Permission isn’t something to wait for. You will die old and unhappy. It’s what you grant yourself , to change, to explore, to imagine a better version of your reality. And when you do, the distance between who you are and who you’re allowed to be becomes smaller, not because the world has changed, but because you’ve changed your relationship with it.
