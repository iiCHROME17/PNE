# God, The Original Artist

## Transcendence

Most people hear transcendence and think of it as overcoming humanity or escaping from suffering. That is mere fantasy. There is enough evidence in different spiritual frameworks to say that suffering is a part of the human condition. The day you stop suffering is the day you stop being a human.

Transcendence is not escaping from the human condition. It is finding meaning within it.

I believe to find meaning inside of the human condition is to become what one could call a living parable.

## The Prayer

Prayer, in the eyes of many, has long been understood as a static moment. Simply to kneel before God and speak. That is a prayer, but not the only kind.

In its essence, prayer is a state of authentic self-exposure. It is the revealing of oneself before God and, equally, before oneself. A moment of vulnerable wrestle; desire, achievement, limitation and failure alike. A person in prayer is a person in their most authentic state, most detached from performance.

There is the closed prayer. This is the private audience with God. The only witnesses are God and the one praying. It can take the form of journaling, a private prayer session, a quiet moment held alone.

Perhaps there is a way to live in perpetual prayer. This is through open prayer. We most recognise this as art. The authentic exposure is encapsulated aesthetically, but the source material is the same. A song, a painting or a poem. Each draws from a person’s authentic reserve to produce something genuinely vulnerable. The form may change, the honesty does not.

To live in unending prayer is to turn one’s life into a piece of art.

## The Living Parable

To define a parable, you must understand it is a story whose meaning is interpreted not given. The lesson received is inferred, not declared outright. Consider the Good Samaritan. A man helps someone unconditionally despite them being Jewish, this conflicted Samaritan interest.

The power of a parable like that is you need nothing to receive it. You don’t have to be a Christian or believe in God at all to hear the Good Samaritan and make meaning out of it. There is no entry fee. It’s available to all.

Art functions the same. There is no entry free to interpreting a painting. You don’t need to be a Christian, Buddhist or anything to stand in front of a Caravaggio painting and arrive at something true to your lived experience.

If we look at Christ symbolically (so from a seccular angle), he wasn’t just the man who told parables. He was a living parable himself. A story about full self-actualisation and integration. What happens when a grown man retains the moral instinct of a child, the empathy and openness both.

In that sense, Jesus Christ became living art. Interpretable, misunderstood and still generative centuries after his death. The same way his parables went misunderstood by his own disciples, so did his life, leading to his death. Misunderstanding is a part of the human condition. Our cognition is finite, of course we will always misunderstand things and go misunderstood ourselves.

That shouldn’t stop anyone from creating their own living parable. Whether that’s music, painting or literature. Whatever form it takes, if there’s something honest about the human condition inside it, that’s all it requires to be valid.

Sincerety is the only price of admission.

## Real World Example

Now I have a controversial example. Again, it’s Kanye West, in the song Runaway.

Runaway is a song where Kanye West is brutally honest. He admits to his wrongdoings without moralising or asking for forgiveness. He gives no excuses. The song acknowledges his failures, his ego and the damage both have caused to his lover. He also admits he still holds a lot of affection for her, whether Amber Rose initially or Kim K during their divorce, the underlying emotional truth is the same.

It’s not confession. It’s witness.

That distinction matters. Confession seeks forgiveness. Witness just honestly reports. Kanye isn’t apologising nor is he glorifying himself as a bastard either. He’s showing us the honest experience of being the way he is. The lesson is never stated outright, it doesn’t need to be.

It’s simply “ do not become me. Stay humble and cherish who you have.”

That is what makes Runaway Kanye’s own parable. In singing it, he becomes one.

He is a grown man holding up a story from his life for other people to interpret and learn from. This doesn’t make him a saint by any margin. Just a man honest enough about their own condition that their life begins to instruct others.

## The Infinite Imagination

So then who exactly is God? That’s probably the next question.

God is closer to an artist rather than the concept of a floating man living in the clouds. There is a study called Panentheism that suggests God exists inside every creation but exceeds them too. To make it simple to understand, an artist can create a painting and that painting contains a fragment of the artist, not the entire artist. A whole gallery will not exhaust the artist’s mind either. Life is the gallery and God is the artist here.

An important distinction I should make is there are two seperate concepts that come into play.

The first is Divine Imagination. This is infinte possibility. An imagination far greater than any human one. It contains everything that has happened, everything happening now and everything that could happens. This includes evil. That includes slavery,genocide and war. The imagination holds ALL of it.

The second being Divine Will. This is what God actually wants. It is entirely distinct from imagination. It works no different to how our minds work. I can imagine something terrible without wanting it to happen. The thought and the intention are not the same thing. That is how an omnipotent God remains all-loving. The capacity to imagine evil does not make you evil.

The Divine exists in everyone and everything,. When a person disconnects from the will towards good and the childlike unselfconsciousness, evil finds its opening. Not as something God sent, something in the absence of the Divine Will.

## What is Evil

Look at the story of Adam and Eve, “The Fall of Man.” and the first to sin.

Reading it symbolically tells a far more useful story than arguing over whether it happened literally. The story is trying to say something about the human condition.

Adam and Eve do not fall simply because of disobedience. Something else happens first. After eating the fruit, the first thing they do is notice their own nudity and feel the need to hide.

Before that moment they were unselfconscious. They simply existed, without the sense that their existence required justification. The closest comparison we have to that state is a child. A child can run around naked without shame because they do not see their existence as something to judge. They simply are.

In the Genesis narrative itself, Adam and Eve are described as “naked and not ashamed” before the fall, a state often interpreted as innocence and openness before God and one another.

So the fall begins when shame enters the picture.

Shame tells a person that something about their existence is wrong. Once that idea captures perspective, people begin hiding parts of themselves the same way Adam and Eve did.

When that fracture happens, it becomes easier to justify harming others too. People try to resolve an internal conflict externally. They attempt to fix a perceived void in that actually sits in the relationship between the self and God.

People often describe evil as disobedience. But disobedience is usually downstream of something deeper.

More often, it begins with shame.

When someone believes their authentic self is unacceptable, they disconnect from it. That disconnection produces the cruelty we see in the world. It may also explain why hell is often described as “separation from God.” The separation begins long before death. It begins when a person becomes separated from themselves.

Animals do not carry this burden. Trees are not ashamed of growing. Insects are not ashamed of eating carcasses. They simply follow their nature.

Humans are the only beings that can become ashamed of their own nature.

And when that happens, evil becomes possible.

## Free Will & Obedience

So why would we have free will just to have to obey something?

The answer is that it has little to do with obedience in the first place. Imagine an artist trying to put every thought onto a canvas at once; no medium, no palette, no structure, no guiding concept. What would you see?

Chaos. Noise. Nothing coherent.

The raw possibility of the mind does not automatically produce beauty. Creativity is defined by constraint. A painter chooses a canvas, a palette, a subject. A haiku has only seventeen syllables. Scat-singing forbids the use of words entirely.

Those limits don’t restrict creativity, they guide it.

Human freedom works like the artist’s imagination. We are capable of imagining and doing almost anything, but without meaningful constraints, beauty does not emerge. Chaos and suffering take root instead. This is where evil finds its footing.

Obedience is not about strict rule-following or literalism. It is not blind submission. It is alignment — choosing to manifest flourishing rather than destruction. Compassion, humility, and care are constraints that guide freedom in this direction without controlling it.

God’s imagination contains all possibility, even the terrible ones. But imagination and will are distinct. Just because something can be imagined does not mean it is desired. Human beings have the free will to bring any of these divine possibilities into reality. Free will makes authenticity possible. Can an artist express themselves if they have no authentic inner world?

Freedom and constraint. Imagination and structure. These are the principles that explain a great artist and they also explain the greatest artist. Their art is life itself.

To truly relate to God, is likely to create from the same tension.

## Your Craft is Your Eden

We will never fully recover the unselfconscious childlike state. A certain level of shame is now simply a part of being human. You can’t roam unclothed in most places in the world without sanction. It’s just part of our condition now.

But we can recover moments of it. That is what craft is for.

Ecclesiastes is direct about this. Everything is meaningless. But not the joy of your food, your companionship and your craft. Those are the exceptions. Those are where life becomes worth the suffering it asks of you.

When you paint for the love of painting itself, you forget to judge yourself. That forgetting, however brief, is the closest experience of divine presence. Whatever generative action you are most passionate about, the one where the process rewards itself by simply happening, is your personal experience of Eden.

A child draws to express something true in that moment, however crude it is. They want it on the fridge as an offering, not a reward. It’s a proof of life. “I made this and I have no shame in the fact I did.” That is the whole concept of transcendence summarised.

To become a child again who draws something and wants it hung on the fridge.

It doesn’t have to be a drawing. It can be any craft. As long as it is yours and carries something sincere about your experience of being alive.

That’s all that’s needed.
