# The UK Bully Culture

## The Trap of Templates

I think it’s fair to say we’re getting bored of content and creation from the UK. It feels like it’s producing the same creators, the same everything. There are probably around five to ten main TikTokers, two main podcasts and three main streamers. Every new creator becomes a variation of what already works.

Literally just search up “We all have that one legendary GC that fell off.”

This isn’t only TikTok. It’s fashion brands, music (which I’ll admit is doing well currently), art and entire creative scenes. The UK has a weird fetish for sticking to templates, and if you don’t, you’re mocked off the internet.

People complain the UK is bad vibes without interrogating why. We never ask what the problem is. To me, maybe it’s the way we treat each other that kills our own creativity.

The UK has become averse to novelty. Experimenting doesn’t work well here. When you try something new, it doesn’t become a movement. It becomes a meme.

It’s like creativity in the UK isn’t an orientation. It’s a status symbol.

## The Stitch-and-Mock Template

Ask ten people their idea of the typical US TikToker. They’ll probably respond with variation. Ask ten people their idea of the typical UK TikToker and they’ll probably point to the Stitch-and-Mock template.

Someone does something weird, different or a bit too much of being themselves, and suddenly there are thirty different TikTokers stitching and mocking them. It appears a lot of UK content requires turning people into lolcows for either being vulnerable or having the audacity to be themselves.

So if a dominant part of online culture in the UK is effectively bullying, how are we to gain any novelty? Personality becomes a crime and conformity becomes a virtue.

I think it might be here where a large chunk of our creativity faces such entropy and stagnation. The dominant templates reward a negative posture towards individuality. That socially influences against self-expression. Who wants to experiment if they’re at risk of being turned into the butt of a joke instead of the leader of their own movement?

By having one of the most dominant templates in UK online culture mock expression, it reinforces itself but also a sense of detachment and exploration adversity.

We bully ourselves out of our own fun.

## The Shame Threshold

Americans are constantly mocked by the British. Too loud, too overzealous, too shameless. They’re cringe.

But it’s that low shame threshold that produces personalities. Those personalities become templates. New templates. The US has multiple different templates running simultaneously. That is to say, there are multiple micro-movements in US online culture.

There was an American teenager who made a YouTube video called “I can rap faster than Eminem.” He uploaded it and got bullied for years, even till today. He became a meme and his confidence became a punchline.

But the same cultural norms that produced his confidence produced a very similar “experiment.” The willingness to put yourself out there at 14, that shamelessness—it made Kodak Black. He was rapping and uploading videos at the same age. No, even younger.

And now he’s a global superstar.

The more willing a culture is to tolerate producing memes and failures, the more capable it is to produce successes and stars. If you have a low tolerance for failure, you have a low threshold for success.

We mock attempts so viciously that people stop trying.

## Intrinsic & Extrinsic Ambition

I think there are two forms of ambition: intrinsic and extrinsic.

Intrinsic ambition is ambition that is fuelled by one’s internal realities. It’s a dedication to somebody’s personal values. Extrinsic ambition is fuelled by social approval, the desire to be relatable or desirable more than to inhabit oneself fully.

Americans arguably have higher rates of intrinsic ambition. That’s what my intuition tells me. Their motivations are more likely to fulfil themselves, their values and their personal vision.

The UK could be said to suffer from the latter. Our motivations often are to fulfil social positions, be socially coherent or be the most desirable. So we chase trends, we follow what works and we stick to what we think is safe.

America creates trailblazers all the time. Kanye West. Virgil Abloh. IShowSpeed. Rihanna. Pharrell. Kendrick Lamar. That’s the result of intrinsic ambition. There are only very few examples off the top of my head for the UK, such as FKA Twigs. I admit I’m liable to bias.

IShowSpeed was seen as cringe and corny for years. We all thought he was annoying. Yet his environment could tolerate him. Now he’s one of the most respectable streamers. He did a world tour through Africa that changed a lot of social perception around it. He had to survive years of mockery first. It’s reasonable to argue that if he were in the UK, he wouldn’t have survived this phase. He may have been shamed into conformity or silence.

## Risk-Aversion

The UK has a high risk-aversion culture. That is what is making us boring.

When you can’t take risks, you can’t really be creative. If you can only be creative insofar as you know it’ll be approved, you’ll never make anything truly new. You won’t be a trailblazer. You’ll just repeat a formula.

That’s generative in a way we know. Not in a new way entirely.

I think Bully Culture is a function inside of our risk-aversion. We shame people for trying something different because different is risky. Conformity is safe.

Nobody who really changed a paradigm played it safe. We romanticise people like Kanye West but ignore how he spent the majority of his twenties being perceived as crazy and obsessive. We look up to figures like Virgil Abloh but ignore the fact that he was an outsider to fashion and had to sustain himself as an outcast for years.

Safe doesn’t make new movements. It doesn’t create culture. It reinforces it. The culture we’re reinforcing is one that prizes legibility and desirability before self-expression. That’s not only boring, it’s also isolating.

## The London Creative

London is the UK’s creative capital. What goes in London becomes the benchmark for everywhere else in the UK.

But that’s been compromised.

The “London Creative Scene,” if there truly is one, has collapsed into performance. These aren’t creatives who make anything new. They’re people who use the idea of being a “creative” purely as a status signal. It’s something to make you more “niche” and elevate yourself above other people. It’s all just ego.

But when creativity is about being right or being desirable to everyone else, it collapses your own potential tenfold. Real creativity, culturally lasting creativity, requires being able to be wrong to everyone else. It requires risk. The risk of failure, mockery or being misunderstood.

When your creativity requires a round of applause before you even make it, you’ve lost. You’re not creating. You’re performing.

So when the London Creative sets the bar for what it means to be a creative in the UK, it caps cultural potential. Being a creative should be the most non-conformist relational identity you can inhabit. So to co-opt it into some form of social media display of desirability? You slap yourself and the culture in the face.

## What Is It We’re Missing?

We don’t have multiple movements. That is the issue. We have very few main movements everyone must stick to. When that movement gets boring, we come online and complain about being bored instead of creating something new.

We have a collective closed mind and we complain about the products of our own process.

America has size and other factors on its side, sure. But they also have a culture that tolerates experimentation. A culture that tolerates failure. They can tolerate people being themselves even when it’s illegible, cringe or downright a failure.

We don’t. We have this closed-minded culture that mocks novelty on sight. Experimentation is ammunition for mockery and conformity is our highest virtue.

And then we wonder why everything feels so plain.

## A Way Out

This isn’t a moral judgement on the UK. It’s a judgement on how we treat each other and especially our creatives and experimental spirits.

“If you want these crazy ideas, stages, music and ways of thinking, chances might be it comes from a crazy person.” - Kanye West

New movements, new templates and new creativity require more open-mindedness. The UK needs to get over this high-shame bully culture that punishes people for even just trying to be themselves.

Creativity is not a social performance. It is an actual life orientation requiring genuine passion for exploration. Actual play and actual risk. Right now, we’re risk-averse. That’s always going to get boring.

The UK has strengths over America. I’m not saying they’re better. Not at all. But when it comes to creative novelty, America is ahead because it is more receptive to difference.

We do not tolerate mess. We mock it, stitch it, turn it into a lolcow.

Then we come sit on the internet and cry nothing’s new.
