# The Experimenter's Mind

## The Beginner’s Mind

In Zen Buddhism, there is a mindset known as “Beginner’s Mind.” This refers to an attitude of openness, eagerness and the lack of preconception when approaching a subject. This is to behave like it’s the first time experiencing a phenomenom, even if it is not.

“In the beginner’s mind there are many possibilities, but in the expert’s there are a few.”

In practical terms, this is non-attachment to being right . What happens as we age is we begin to predict and expect outcomes. Our behaviours begin to be shaped by the chase for correctness. It’s not that the world becomes less possible; we simply begin to rely on our need to be right more than our curiosity about what we don’t know.

There is more possible in the beginner’s mind because they have the ability to explore what they don’t know. Experience becomes a sandbox. The Expert, on the other hand, can only defend the position they’ve taken. The only possibilities that exist are the ones that validate their choices, including the choice to appear right.

The Beginner’s mind implies something. It is not that most people lack intelligence or the capacity for it. What people may lack is the epistemic humility in order to sustain the Beginner’s Mind. What stops people from being able to engage with the world and their lives, is their perceived threat of being wrong about it.

Life has seemingly more to offer than many of us realise, but when the ego grows and hardens, perception becomes narrow. It is that collapse in perception and perspective that confines people to a life of limited possibility.

## The World Of Information, The Self Of Signals

Where Beginner’s Mind emphasises seeing the world with a childlike clarity, the experimenter’s mind treats that clarity scientifically.

If the world contains an unquantifiable number of possibilities, we need a way to operate within it.

How do we choose when faced with a million choices?

The Experimenter’s Mind requires seeing the world as streams of information and the self as a conduit. You must divorce raw experience from the narrative (the predictions) your mind imposes on it. Everything becomes signal flowing through you. There should be no immediate judgment, no attachment. Experience stops being reality to react to and becomes input to process.

Experience does not equal truth. It’s a signal. Information about how the world works. Information about how you work. This is what allows direction in a world oversaturated with misdirection.

From here, identity should be emergent . Do not pick your identity. Discover it from following the highest quality signals.

In cognitive science, we know the brain constantly predicts and filters signals, constructing our models of the world. The Experimenter’s Mind believes that signal is more important than protecting the model.

To see yourself as a conduit is to emphasise flow over control. When you encounter wisdom, perspective or understanding, you accept you are not the source. You are the synthesiser. Creativity, insight and action emerge from how you combine and interpret input.

Consider two people experiencing a moment of intense anger.

The first reacts. The emotion drives them towards a poor, potentially harmful action.

The second observes. They treat the emotion as data about themselves. They realise the anger stems from a betrayal of their own values. This helps them identify what they care about. Where they’re passionate and suggests how to move toward it.

It’s the same experience. Different relationship to the signal.

## The Identity as Systems

One thing I learned from computer science is to think in systems. I played a video game called Disco Elysium which made me realise even identity is an assortment of systems.

The Experimenter’s Mind should make you understand this: your habits, beliefs, emotions and creativity are all interacting subsystems.

The way software is developed is through iteration and feedback. You begin with a concept and then refine it through multiple versions. Many of them will be incomplete and bugged. Developers do not stop experimenting until they reach quality assurance standards.

Identity should be treated the same.

Think of yourself like a computer. Each part of your identity, your race, your gender, your belief systems is pre-installed software. Left unexamined, you’ll never know if these applications contain bugs that degrade your system performance, wellbeing and creativity.

The only way to optimise identity fully requires iterative experimentation and feedback. Use the signals the world provides to understand whether these parts of your identity make you feel more or less aligned with who you want to be.

The goal isn’t to delete or deny parts of your identity, you cannot outrun having one. It’s understanding when it’s controlling you and rewriting the code so you control it.

Are you controlling your identity? Or is it controlling you?

Consider: what part of you needs to be seen as legible, coherent or agreeable. Is that need serving you or defending itself?

## Divine Intelligence

The concept of Iterative Experimentation is not new. It isn’t a human invention. The Experimenter’s Mindset is arguably a form of Divine Intelligence. To strip that of its mysticism, it exists naturally within the world, with God (or The Universe) seemingly being The Experimenter.

Take a look at wild nature. All of these different trees, rivers, flowers and animals. They didn’t just arrive on Earth looking as they did. The most beautiful parts of nature were products of process. Iterative, naturally occurring experimentation process.

Look at my favourite insect for example. The Orchid Mantis. It did not start off as the Orchid Mantis. Neither did whatever Divine Source say “Let there be an Orchid Mantis.”

What occurred is there was an ancestor to the Orchid Mantis and the natural world committed it to experimentation. The ancestors that resembled orchids survived and reproduced -that's the positive feedback. The negative feedback was death. Over centuries, these flower-like traits got more and more reinforced.

There were multiple other subspecies that existed from the same ancestor but most of them died out. Through centuries-long iterative experimentation, the Orchid Mantis' was produced without ever gaining genetic information from an Orchid.

The key here is this: It took an unquantifiable amount of iterations and failures for the Orchid Mantis to exist today. But those failures never stopped the experiment, they were corrective fuel. Personal evolution, whether that is creative or general, requires experimentation sustained through failure.

That’s the focus of the Experimenter’s Mind. Emergence can prove superior to intention. If you let go of intended outcomes, you might find the emergent outcome was far greater than you could’ve imagined.

## The Ego

So if iterative experimentation is natural and universal, why is it rare? It’s accessible, so one could argue it shouldn’t be rare. The answer is because the experimenter’s mind isn’t complex. It’s resisted. The mindset makes one a student of life. The ego makes one think they understand life already.

The ego isn’t inherently evil. Everyone has an ego, whether big or small. It’s the unchecked ego that becomes a problem. It needs to be right more than it needs to learn. The ego is all about reliably predicting reality, not reinterpreting it.

It is within that need to reliably predict reality where attachment to outcomes poisons the experiment. The brain experiences a phenomenon called cognitive dissonance whenever the ego’s prediction fails to align with the actual reality of the situation.

This cognitive dissonance is a mental discomfort typically resolved through defensiveness or shame projection. This stops people from correcting their prediction models and embracing the experimenter’s mind because they rely too heavily on their old models, even when they’re not beneficial.

The experimenter’s mind requires negative feedback: failure, mistakes. The unintegrated ego interprets that as identity threat. Failure threatens the perceivable competence of your identity.

The mindset also requires positive feedback: success. But the unintegrated ego treats the validation of success not like a signal, but the destination itself , opting out of experimentation and risk.

Together, the unintegrated ego doesn’t allow any new information in, only information that satisfies its prediction models already.

Most people say they want growth. What many people actually want is proof they were right. The experimenter’s mind wants truth more than comfort. This is why beginner’s mind is needed. You cannot experiment if you cannot be wrong.

Without epistemic humility, you’ll be uncomfortable trying to experiment.

## First-Order, Second-Order

The Experimenter’s Mind is not about erasing identity. It is easy to misunderstand non-attachment to experience as detachment or nihilism. The Experimenter’s Mind must be contained in a framework that holds both stability and iteration.

A First-Order Identity is the embodied self. This part is who you are. It’s where your emotions, your survival needs and cultural grounding exist.

A Second-Order Identity is the observer within you. It’s who is asking questions. It’s who identifies patterns, experiments and designs systems.

They both need one another. Creativity and growth require a balance between the first-order and the second-order.

First-Order Dominance means everything becomes personal. You become bound to emotional reactivity and cannot observe patterns because you are them. There is no distance, no iteration. You’re enslaved to being defined by your experience.

Second-Order Dominance is subtler, however equally as destructive. It turns you into an observer in your own life. There’s no investment, no stakes. First-Order makes things matter. But if nothing matters, there’s nothing you can experiment with.

First-Order Identity is needed to have preferences, passion and direction. Second-Order Identity is what makes adjustments to the parts of First-Order that don’t serve you.

The Experimenter’s Mind isn’t about denying oneself. It is about allowing oneself to be unfinished in the process of becoming.

## This Is Not New

The experimenter’s mind is not a new mindset or perspective whatsoever. I haven’t invented anything new, I’ve only proposed new articulation for it. Many high-level creatives live it intuitively and even speak of it with less academic rigour. Tyler, The Creator sums up the whole concept well: “Create like a child, then edit like a scientist.”

The contribution here is language, not discovery.

If one is to look at how creatives such as Virgil Abloh created, it’s clear his work was treated as iterable systems. The movement from Pyrex Vision to Off-White was less design-heavy and more systemic. Virgil seemingly concerned himself with the system of attention, value, and production. The conceptual difference between Pyrex and Off-White isn’t too distinct; it is iteration that made the difference.

But what was Virgil Abloh’s  iterative system? He’s already told us. It is the 3% rule. Take a piece of art and modify it by only 3%. In doing that, you make a culturally viable product. It is systems-thinking made into a process. You do not need reinvention or grand innovation all the time he suggests. You can just remix what already exists. It is within this process Off-White was born.

Looking at creatives such as Kanye, Tyler, and A$AP Rocky, it also becomes clear how their identities are emergent, not fixed. All three artists do not rigidly abide by one identity but evolve their perspective over their discographies and aestheticise it into a product. Their identity emerges from experimented experience, not social affirmation or desirability.

This shows in differences between “The College Dropout” Kanye west compared to “Yeezus” Kanye. In “Long Live A$AP” A$AP Rocky compared to “DON’T BE DUMB” A$AP Rocky. Even the edgy, controversial “Goblin” Tyler, the Creator compared to the more emotional “FLOWER BOY” Tyler, The Creator.

These people aren’t special purely because they’re talented. There’s an abundance of talent. What’s added that makes them truly special is that they can iterate without ego attachment (or through ego collapse). Without the experimenter’s mind, it is arguable their talent may have gone under-appreciated or less sought after.

But this mindset isn’t mystical or genetic. You can learn it.

It’s a posture toward experience. A conscious, repeated choice to allow reality to emerge rather than fit within our preconceived predictions.

## The Experimenter’s Mind

This is the experimenter’s mind. It is not mystical, yet it feels transcendent. It’s far less complex than it actually sounds. It is, in plain English, disciplined humility applied over time . The willingness to let reality shape meaning instead of confirming the one you’ve designed.

This isn’t philosophy I’ve written. It’s an epistemic posture to practise . The more you practise it, the more you may believe is possible.

Things only seem impossible because we trust our limited understanding more than we trust our faith.

Creativity stops being something you wait for and becomes something iterated toward. Identity stops being a performance to protect a narrative and becomes the system of you which you refine. You’re not defending who you’ve been. You’re discovering who you’re becoming.

Faith stops being rigid doctrine and becomes trust in the process of emergence. You believe that with sustained faith, what emerges for you will serve you well.

The experimenter’s mind at no point erases difficulty or suffering. It recontextualises it. Emotions stop being endpoints about who we are and what we’re worth. They become signals . Data points about what works, what doesn’t, and where to go next.

All it needs is the ability to stop and say, “I know I don’t know.” To stop defending the way in which you predict the world.

Teams that only play defence, never score.
