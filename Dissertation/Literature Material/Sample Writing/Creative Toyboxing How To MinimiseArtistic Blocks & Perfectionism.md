# Creative Toyboxing: How To Minimise Artistic Blocks & Perfectionism

## Introduction

In most creative industries, all artists suffer from two distinct issues: art blocks and perfectionism. This, alongside other issues, leads to homogenisation. Perfectionism can appear as the absence of ideas; it’s the paralysis that exists when expression is judged before it is executed. Perfectionism may feel like severe discipline, but truthfully it is just fear. This fear could be the fear of being seen, being flawed, or being different.

Homogenisation is the natural consequence of both art blocks and perfectionism; when creators no longer create from impulse but from reference, recycling the same safe gestures because risk has become socially expensive. These three inputs feed into each other. The artist silences themselves, polishes the silence, and then replicates what has been said, done, or heard already.

Toyboxing is a creative philosophy and practice against this circuit. It is a system to return to the child mind, where play precedes approval and art reclaims its raw psychic function.

## The Source

The source of creativity is innately childlike. Any time you create something, that is your inner child creating it. Using psychodynamic terms, your id is the captain of your creations. What happens as we age is our superego -the inner critic or the mental representation of your parents and inner audience -strengthens. This is what causes such paralysis in many creative people’s lives. The superego does not give the id chance to just exist, blocking one’s access to the inner child.

The importance of having connection to this so-called “inner child” is incredible. To be able to tap into this side of your psyche means you will be able to create without thinking about abstract concepts.

Think about how a child might draw. If a child feels happy, they draw a picture of what makes them happy. It’s often crude, ugly, but heartfelt. This is pure id work happening. Children have very poor ability to categorise, judge, or reject information. Everything to children is raw material. This makes their work raw emotional expressions.

As we age, we learn these exact abilities. They are comparative measures. These form what people think of as good and bad -what we call standards. When you listen too harshly to these measures, eventually you fall into paralysis, perfectionism, or creative anhedonia (where nothing you create feels like it has you in it).

For an unbalanced superego-id connection, it may be useful to read: The Sensitive Mind: A Conceptual Framework for Self-Integration

## Psychology of Emotions

Your emotions and experiences are fuel for creativity. Even things you don’t notice affect how you think and create. This is discussed widely by psychologists like Freud and Jung. Our minds store thoughts and feelings we don’t fully understand yet, argued by Freud. Jung said we all have a “shadow” part of ourselves we haven’t fully faced. These hidden thoughts and feelings often show up in creative works and interpretations that we might not realise until later.

For instance, I created a story about 5 years ago and started a rewrite of it 2 years ago. I created a character named Silver. At first, she was just a mysterious, cool assassin figure. After a difficult romantic situation, I realised I began to integrate aspects of the other person into Silver. This resulted in Silver now being someone who has a legitimate fear of vulnerability with a desperate need for friendship. This gave the character real depth and emotion, turning her from a surface-level “cool character” into someone with actual humanlike emotional resonance.

Understanding psychology for many creative fields gives creative work emotional intensity. When ideas are based on real feelings or experiences, even subconscious ones, the result is artwork that actually feels alive.

## Precontextualisation

Precontextualisation is the fancy way of saying that not every idea needs to have a clear meaning right away. Some ideas need to wait for the right context before they make sense.

Think of it as collecting puzzle pieces. You might have a piece that doesn’t fit anywhere yet, but later, it completes an amazing picture. In game design, for instance, a character, story element, or game mechanic might sit in the “toybox” until the right moment in the world or story makes it meaningful. In fashion, a fabric, texture, or design might not make immediate sense but could become perfect in a later collection.

Precontextualisation also mirrors how our minds work. Sometimes we absorb ideas, theses, or patterns subconsciously. Things tend to hold up mirrors to certain parts of ourselves we know exist but cannot articulate or process. These things only make sense when we’re ready to understand them. This is why you might realise months or years later why you loved a certain story or character as a kid. Precontextual art allows for experimentation and play without worrying about whether it “works” immediately.

To simplify this explanation, think of it childishly. Children don’t know context at all. They know immediacy. They create art purely for emotional expression. Believing that this emotional expression may lead to a product eventually is what may help an artist overcome blocks and perfectionism. Everything made doesn’t need to fit a specific purpose; it can just be a natural emotional expression experiment. Creations can exist before the context they’re used in.

Another illustrative example: I drew a rough character one day. I had no reason to draw other than I had finished work quite late and was too awake to go to bed, too sleepy to create anything of detail. I ended up drawing a cartoon figure just because. The only notable detail was his eyes were red and his pupils were hearts. I made this for no apparent reason and called him the “Serotonin Junkie.” Eventually, when it was time to make a game, I found myself looking to this random obscure drawing as a reference for the main antagonist, in which I then constructed a context for the drawing’s existence, months after actually making it.

## The Philosophy of Toyboxing

Creative Toyboxing is the practice of collecting fragments of yourself. These are emotions, ideas, experiences, dreams, and crucially, mistakes. It is giving these things a container to live in until they find their meaning.

Instead of forcing yourself to create masterpieces, learn to collect without judgement. Every sketch, thought, or emotional moment becomes a toy. One day, this toy may become part of something larger. When toyboxing, the question isn’t “Is this good?” but “Does this feel like it carries a piece of me?”

If the answer is yes, it goes in the box.

### Stop Evaluating and Allowing Evaluation

We’re trained to evaluate too soon. Society prizes finished products, not fragments. The Toybox system doesn’t abide by that effect. Most people throw away their half-formed ideas because they don’t fit right now.

Creativity is not a straight line. It is an orbit. Ideas circle around a central emotional truth -I call this the “nucleus” -until they eventually click into alignment.

When you stop seeing unfinished work as failure, you stop fearing your own process. You realise what society calls bad ideas are just premature ideas. Ideas that have not met their context. The only thing that makes any work “bad” is if it isn’t truthful to the emotional nucleus of its creator. Unfinished work isn’t wasted time; it’s stored potential.

### Emotional Mechanics

Toyboxing, essentially, is emotional alchemy. It’s not just archiving concepts; you’re storing feelings in symbolic form. When you put something in the toybox, you’re telling yourself that “This emotion matters enough to keep, even if I don’t understand its integration yet.”

This is what gives Toyboxing psychological power. It teaches emotional patience. Instead of forcing closure on pain, confusion, or uncertainty, you preserve fragments of them until time, perspective, or experience gives them meaning. Creativity then becomes not a therapy session but a continuing dialogue with your evolving self.

### The 3% Rule

This rule is not original. It’s stolen. Like most creativity is. It is a reinterpretation of Virgil Abloh’s rule of creativity. He said make a 3% change to a pre-existing concept to make something new and culturally valuable.

You can do that, and also capture 3% of what you feel each day. That’s enough to keep your creative and emotional energy flowing without burning out. Small, consistent inputs compound. The tortoise beat the hare, remember? A few honest fragments a day becomes hundreds over a year. When it’s time to create, you’ll have emotional capital to spend. Just collect a small portion of your feelings or inner dialogue each day and turn them into a symbol for whatever creative industry you exist within.

### Cross-Contamination of Creativity

Creativity thrives when you expose it to foreign environments. Bacteria mutate and strengthen when cross-contaminated in a lab; creative systems evolve when they come into contact with new mediums, methods, and industries.

This isn’t a call to abandon your main craft. It means learning to translate insights between creative languages. To extract the essence of a discipline and reapply it elsewhere.

Most creative ruts are a problem of creative inbreeding. You repeat the same structures, habits, and aesthetic choices until your work becomes predictable. This is over-specialisation.

The more you isolate creation into one domain, the more fragile your imagination becomes. This means your id -the inner child in you -weakens and the superego gains favour, leading to perfectionism, blocks, and homogenisation as said before.

Cross-contamination is the antidote. It introduces new “genetic material” into your creative ecosystem.

Another example is that I enjoy game design. It is not until this year, being 2025, that I decided to take a break from creating games, which I would never get too far into. I decided to play with visual arts and philosophy, which deepened my ability to create good stories in games. My course in university, Computer Science, also made me think of systems. The result is I combined Computer Science-level creativity to learn how to spot and conceptually remix pre-existing game systems and mechanics alongside philosophy and psychology-level understandings of human nature to create better game concepts with strong stories and mechanics to support them.

Chefs learn rhythm, constraint, and sensory layering. Programmers learn systems thinking and efficiency. Fashion designers learn silhouette, proportion, and emotional tone through texture. Each of these insights can be abstracted down to a universal creative principle. Once abstracted, they become portable atoms you can drop into any Toybox.

## The Architecture of the Toybox

Your toybox is not a random collection of ideas. It is an emotional microcosm with four main components:

The Nucleus: The emotional or conceptual core around which all creative fragments orbit. It is the gravitational center of your creative identity.

The Atom: The individual fragment: an idea, motif, sound, colour, or memory that carries emotional charge.

The Toy: A preliminary artwork or expression that gives form to an atom. Toys are precontextual; they exist before purpose, serving as prototypes of emotion or thought.

The Play Scenario: The dynamic experiment, or creative “game,” where multiple toys are combined to discover new relationships and emergent meaning.

The goal is equilibrium where experimentation generates insight without collapsing into incoherence, and structure provides direction without suffocating originality.

Too much chaos leads to irreplicability: ideas so wild they can’t be developed, sold, or sustained. Bad for artists who want to eat food and sleep under a roof.

Too much order breeds creative safety: predictable, derivative works that fail to stand out.

The Toybox, therefore, isn’t merely a storage system for inspiration; it’s a metabolic process that keeps your creative life balanced, digesting chaos into coherence, and order into innovation.

“Every idea you love is orbiting something deeper -a need, a memory, or a truth you haven’t fully faced.”

You can have multiple nuclei. One might be freedom, one might be loss. Another identity. Another control. It’s personal and permanent: a part of you existentially. While projects come and go, these emotional nuclei hardly change. It’s your creative signature. Without understanding your nucleus, you risk creating work that feels hollow or borrowed, because it lacks the gravitational pull that makes art feel urgent and alive.

Every character, every arc and power -they all revolve around different interpretations, representations, and manifestations of suffering, with the main character being an existentialist reacting to these different worldviews. Even then, I recursively realised this was inspired by Naruto Uzumaki, showing my love for the character was precontextual.

### Atoms

“Every idea, no matter how small, carries a piece of your emotional fingerprint.”

Atoms are fragments: ideas, words, sketches, references, sensations, and moments. Each atom has two different qualities:

Content: what it is (a song, a phrase, a visual, a mechanic).

Charge: what it feels like.

### Toys

“Everything you consciously make already has a piece of you in it.”

Toys are the first tangible manifestations of your atoms. They are small artworks, sketches, melodies, prototypes. They are to be created without expectation of completion or perfection. A toy is what happens when you say “What if I tried this?” without demanding it to be good.

Every toy carries one or more atoms, brought into form through play. This stage is where imperfection becomes gold. You’re aiming never for polish. You’re capturing an emotion before it fades. Toys can be seen somewhat like Pokémon. The Pokémon is a certain emotional intensity; the Pokéball is the toy that contains it. The Pokéball isn’t forced to store one specific Pokémon, as it can be let out at any time and replaced.

Precontextual toys are vital since they keep creativity alive between major projects. They’re emotional exercises, snapshots of the subconscious, and often the early seeds of your best work. The trick is to make a lot of toys -small, fast, and honest. Don’t hoard emotions; externalise them.

## How To Build A Toybox

### 0. ESRA (External-Self-Referential Analysis)

An extract from more of my writing may help you:

“External-Self-Referential Analysis (ESRA) is a particularly powerful approach to working with art, media, or other emotionally resonant external stimuli. ESRA is a cognitive-metacognitive method in which an individual identifies stimuli (such as paintings, dialogue, events, symbols, or fictional characters) and systematically deconstructs them to uncover the abstract conceptual structures they activate within the self.

At its core, ESRA explicitly engages metacognition: it requires noticing emotional responses, tracing them back to cognitive and subconscious patterns, and reflecting on their origin and significance. This creates a feedback loop between internal awareness and external observation, bridging subconscious emotional patterns and conscious intellectual processing. By naming, analysing, and articulating the internal impact of external stimuli, the individual strengthens higher-order self-awareness, gaining insight into habitual thought patterns, emotional triggers, and latent drives.

For example, consider a personal experience of revisiting a story from one’s past. Rewatching Naruto years after first encountering it, I noticed a profound emotional reaction to the character Naruto Uzumaki: a radical sense of empathy coupled with a conviction toward “ending cycles of suffering.” When first watching it as a child, I could not articulate why I was drawn to him. Using ESRA, I asked metacognitive questions: Why does this character move me? What internal ideals or values does he reflect? How do my responses connect to my own self-concept?

The insight that emerged was that Naruto mirrored a component of my ideal self: the part striving for empathy, moral commitment, and the transformation of suffering into understanding. In other words, an external stimulus (a fictional character) served as a mirror for latent internal values that had remained implicit for over a decade. This illustrates how ESRA allows external phenomena to illuminate internal structures, providing clarity to experiences that were previously unconscious or unarticulated.

This process works bidirectionally:

By learning to articulate why external things move us, ESRA develops the metacognitive skill of externalising internal events: consciously observing, interpreting, and transforming them into intentional creative expression. It functions as both a mirror and a laboratory for metacognitive cultivation, turning sensitivity into structured insight, intentionality, and generative creativity.”

### 1. Excavation

Identify the emotional gravity behind your creativity: your “why.” Ask what emotion, wound, or obsession keeps pulling you back in. Write a single sentence that defines it. This nucleus anchors everything that follows; you’ll revisit and refine it as you evolve. This is usually the trickier part. Be patient with yourself here; the nucleus often reveals itself gradually through the act of creating rather than through pure introspection.

### 2. Collection

Collect fragments that emotionally move you. Sounds, textures, memories, and words. Anything that sparks emotional resonance. Tag them by the kind of charge they give, not a category. Store them loosely in a digital or physical Toybox. Don’t worry too hard about the meaning; they’ll find the context later.

### 3. Construction

Turn one or more atoms into a quick creative expression. This could be a sketch, phrase, mechanic, or sound. Keep it fast, imperfect, and honest. Capture the raw emotion before it fades. Let your inner child here run free and abandon your inner critic. Toys are not meant for evaluation, so do not allow yourself to evaluate.

### 4. Run Scenarios

Combine toys in new ways. Test out emotional chemistry. Mix media, swap context, let randomness be your guide. Document what works and what fails. Both are valuable data. Recycle the failed scenarios as new toys. They may often reveal hidden patterns later or catalyse new processes from your prior experiences of them.

### 5. Equilibrium

Allow chaos to spark discovery, but shape it with structure. Too much chaos breeds confusion; too much order kills surprise. The sweet spot is playful control: where experimentation feels alive but purposeful. Re-enter the cycle. Every round strengthens your creative gravity and emotional clarity.

## Conclusion

Perfectionism and art blocks are not signs of weakness. They are signs that you care deeply about what you make, but have temporarily lost access to the part of you that knows how to play. Toyboxing is the practice of reclaiming that access.

The act of creating is not about producing perfect work. It is about staying in conversation with yourself. Every atom you collect, every toy you build, every failed play scenario: these are all evidence that you are listening to your inner life and giving it form. This is what keeps creativity alive.

The Toybox doesn’t promise that every fragment will become a masterpiece. It promises something more valuable: that nothing you feel, notice, or create will be wasted. Every piece has potential. Every emotion has a place. And when the time is right, when context finally meets content, those precontextual fragments will click into alignment and reveal what they were always meant to become.

I’ve spent years cycling between creative paralysis and creative overflow. The difference now is that I have a system. I don’t wait for inspiration to strike. I collect fragments. I make toys. I run scenarios. And when it’s time to create something substantial, I have an entire toybox of emotional material to draw from. The work feels like me because it is me: stored, preserved, and ready to be reassembled.

You are not blocked. You are not broken. You are simply holding fragments that haven’t found their context yet. Build your Toybox. Fill it without judgement.
