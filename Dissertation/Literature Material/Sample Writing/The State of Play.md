# The State of Play

## Alexander Fleming

The problem I’ve identified with so much of what’s online is it always tells people to set goals. I think society has an odd obsession with the idea that success is a measure of dedication to goals. People are so convinced that fulfilment is a measure of big goals and the motivation to reach them.

I struggle to agree with that.

Literally look at the case of Alexander Fleming. He is the person who discovered penicillin. That’s a revolutionary medical breakthrough he made. Yet it wasn’t something he was actively pursuing when he developed it. Alexander Fleming was experimenting. Penicillin did not make its way into our reality as a result of goal-oriented thinking. It was a result of an accident.

Fleming grew bacteria in petri dishes in 1928. He’d test substances that might block or slow their growth. What ended up happening is he left his lab for a holiday and when he returned, one of his petri dishes contained mould. It was that petri dish that turned out to be Penicillium notatum . The substrate of what becomes the penicillin we use today.

The point of this story is clear. Sometimes the greatest forms of evolution and breakthroughs in our lives aren’t from strict goal following. It’s from openness to experience. Inside that openness, we can explore, investigate and iterate.

Alexander Fleming isn’t the only story of such a breakthrough coming from this kind of openness. In fact, I’ve learned that it’s a cognitive framework shared by certain creatives that explains their divisiveness.

I call it the “State of Play” and it operates on the Fleming principle: Evolution often is best kept emergent. Planned evolution is limited to the scope of our understanding.

## You’ve Seen The State Of Play Before

We’ve all been exposed to people who exist in the State of Play. I’ll forever say it’s not new. We’re accustomed to calling it “experimenting” or being “experimental.” Either way, it’s just an adult’s way of saying they play.

Take A$AP Rocky. He’s probably my favourite example aesthetically for being able to exist in such a clearly childlike space without feeling childish or overly cartoonish. Watch any Rocky music video and you’ll see what I mean. Something like Riot. Rocky’s highly experimental. His album TESTING is just one big experiment, which is to say, TESTING was album-level play.

Another role model of mine is Hideo Kojima. He has the exact same disposition. He also exists in this state. The Metal Gear Solid series is no longer experimental but architectural for many video game mechanics, including cinematics and stealth. But at the time, especially with MGS 1 and 2 , they were heavily experimental, resulting from Kojima playing with his passion for film and turning them into video game mechanics. If you’ve ever played a story-driven game, there’s a heavy chance that somewhere in there is Kojima’s influence.

Both of these creators call it experimenting. That’s the professionally appropriate term for it. But really, they’re just two men who didn’t lose the fundamental ability to play.

## What Does It Mean To Play?

One thing people misunderstand is that being playful means immaturity or taking life unseriously. It’s a complete misinterpretation. Play isn’t childish. Children are just better at embodying the conditions that create it.

In simple terms, play is relational openness. That relation can be to another person, sure. But it can also be with life itself. Play isn’t indulging in chaos. It’s engaging with something without demanding a specific outcome. It’s exploring rather than executing. It’s unselfconscious engagement with whatever’s in front of you.

Adults love to say that making friends is hard. It really isn’t if both parties can embody the state of play. The reason children make friends so easily is because they behave without pretence. There’s no identity to defend. No need for evaluation. No required outcome. Children operate from an unselfconscious lens that allows them to be relationally open and willing to explore meaning, not defend it.

This is why children are so friendly. Adults are just as capable of this playfulness. It just goes from given to cultivated. As Picasso put it: “It took me four years to paint like Raphael but a lifetime to paint like a child.”

## The Mischievous Adult

When children play, it’s celebrated as natural. For a child to treat the world like a sandbox of possibility and believe they can do anything is admirable. If a child says they want to be a police officer, we accept it. The next day if they say they want to be an astronaut, we accept it. If they say they want to be a king or a queen, again we accept it.

Children are naturally ambitious by default. Their lack of self-consciousness means they just want to be the most impressive thing they can think of. They don’t settle for what’s realistic. That’s because they exist naturally in the State of Play. They’re open to possibility. So if they can imagine being something, there’s little to no shame in wanting to be it, even if it’s considered delusional.

But that same sentiment goes nowhere with adults. When a child plays, it’s natural. When an adult plays (without the pretence of “experimenting”), it’s chaos. Something we’re supposed to suppress. My belief is that the purest form of ambition and personal wellbeing is closest to that of a child. It’s just that we learn shame and believe that same openness to possibility is now problematic.

The only time I see playfulness in adults typically excused is in romance, professional roles or under institutional permission. However, is it really play if someone must sanction it for you?

Sometimes, I like to call my own state the “State of Mischief.” This is the idea that one can at any time just decide to stop waiting for some external context or permission for play and simply inhabit it for themselves. If a child plays in a context or way they’re not allowed, it’s called mischief. So I see it as appropriate to think: the child experiences the State of Play and the self-sovereign adult experiences the State of Mischief. They’re all the same, the only difference being social perception.

## Play Loves Structure

A core clarification is that play loves structure. All play is not inherently good, especially if it has no underlying structure.

To return to the State of Play, one needs to integrate chaos, awareness and responsibility. That is how you access the state once you get removed from it. There’s a version of the State of Play that masquerades as the same thing, but it’s more a form of ego regression. A coping mechanism to deal with the pressures of adult life.

There are three criteria I’ve observed to distinguish play from chaos.

1. Does it sustain itself through contextual evolution?

In simple terms, does your playfulness require you to stay the same person? I think playfulness in its true form should emerge alongside your growth, not require you to regress and avoid it. Nor should it force you into emotional reactivity.

2. Does it harm you or others?

This is very simple. Genuine play is generative, not destructive. If you find yourself subtracting from your life or others’ lives, you’re probably being irresponsible and selfish, not playful.

3. Can you sustain it with self-compassion?

This point is key. Genuine play collapses without self-compassion. It is self-compassion that enables a lack of self-consciousness through social adversity. If you are genuine in your playfulness, it will often end up somewhat illegible. If you cannot be compassionate enough towards yourself, you’ll find yourself a victim of projected shame. That collapses into performance, reactivity or chaos quickly. You must become your own compassionate witness because the State of Play is unsustainable without self-compassion.

One thing I will also argue is that maintaining the State of Play absolutely requires spiritual integrity. You need faith. Planned evolution is limited to the scope of your perspective, so to trust emergent evolution, you need faith in the emergent unknown. In the words of Kanye, you need to adopt the greatest spiritual stance and say:

“I know I don’t know.”

## The Evidence This Exists

This isn’t an opinionated article. It’s closer to a meta-analysis in easy English. There are many creatives and visionaries whose processes I’ve studied, and this is just the result of their similarities. Some final examples to illustrate the validity of the State of Play:

Hideo Kojima has evolved through clear constraints: stealth mechanics to cinematic storytelling to strand-type games. He operates from “I know I don’t know,” experimenting during development and letting games teach him what they should be. Each Metal Gear Solid game is mechanically novel, not formulaic. When he was fired from Konami, he didn’t regress. He made Death Stranding . His work often divides fans. MGS2 replaced the protagonist halfway through, which was illegible to many at the time but proved he was playing, not performing for approval.

Tyler, The Creator has evolved dramatically. Goblin was shock rap. Flower Boy became emotionally expressive. Chromakopia explored jazz and R&B. His early work faced massive criticism, but he didn’t regress or defend himself. He just evolved. Each album operates within new constraints, new genres, new lenses. Each one is new play that produces new emergence.

Kanye West literally said “I know I don’t know.” His evolution through constraints is clear: chipmunk soul to 808s to gospel and onwards. 808s sounded “wrong” for 2008 hip-hop. Rapping about emotions during gangster rap eras was illegible to many. But he sustained his play through backlash without regression.

Why this matters is because it shows that this isn’t an abstract theory. It’s a legitimate operating system. The reason all of these creatives are so successful and divisive is because of their playfulness, which does promise illegibility at some point.

But it’s not something reserved for creatives only. Anybody can take up this state. It’s something you cultivate, not something you stumble upon.

You just need to learn openness to experience.
