# The Digital Wild West

## The Wild West

Look at the Wild West as a landscape that was defined by uncertainty and freedom, or chaos if you want to call it that. Conventional rules were thinly enforced and boundaries were constantly shifting. Survival depended on boldness, improvisation and risk-taking for many. It was a world where creativity, both strategic and chaotic, could flourish Only for those willing to digest unpredictability.

That metaphor might describe where creativity is heading now.

What we’re seeing with AI and the collapsing interest in algorithm-centric art and content fits the same frame. Traditional structures, standards and hierarchies are dissolving. The old rules about what gets attention, what gets valued and what gets made are losing their grip. Creators willing to navigate that open frontier have more room than they’ve had in years.

It’s one of the riskiest times to be a creative.

But that’s exactly why it could be the best.

If you do it right.

## The Overcrowded State of Art

We all know what the problem with art and content is. We've reached a point of homogenisation that makes everything uniform. I look at all these fliers and posters online and in-person and know and it's clearly ChatGPT's work. But something worth recognising is that AI did not make art uniform.

Algorithms did.

They’ve been doing this for more than a decade. Instagram. TikTok, Pinterest - all of it. They promote the promise one post may transform you from a starving artist to a renowned online presence. The cost?

Individuality.

All algorithmns are built on mathematical, statistical and quantifiable data. They’re comparative by nature. To succeed, you have to play by their standards. Artists and creators naturally complied. Art became more digestible, technical skill, specatacle and branding became more important than message. For the artist who can’t afford to miss rent, it’s not a moral failing but survival in an unjust system.

These events describe art from the late 2000s to the present day. AI didn’t make art uniform. Artists exploring what the algorithm could promise them did, instead of exploring themselves.

The instagram basquiat clones are the clearest example. People stopped learning from Basquiat’s relationship with his interior and started trying to look like him in the hope of going viral.

They took the aesthetic and left behind the thing that could actually help them - the fact it was the product of someone’s incredibly intimate relationship with themselves.

## We Need Intimacy Again

When I say intimacy I mean something specific. Not yearning or vulnerability for its own sake. I mean conceptual work so particular to one person that it couldn’t have come from anyone else.

Two things make that possible.

The first is absurdism. Not the philosophy, the practical reality of irrational progression. AI produces the most optimal, rational output for any given input. So the human artist's advantage is irrationality.

To be specific when I say “irrational “ I mean work that doesn't follow the logic of what camebefore it. Look at The Regular Show’s episode structure. Look at psychological thrillers like “Enemy (2013.)”. Both feature a kind of irrational progression that feels emotionally coherent even when it makes no rational sense. That coherence is human emotiional intelligence. It can't be reverse engineered.

The second is surrealism. Not Dalí’s work but the actual principle behind it. The unconscious mind, its symbols, its dream logic, its refusal to be neat. AI can hallucinate but it cannot dream meaningfully. Your unconscious is the most intimate part of you and it's also the least replicable. Work rooted there is structurally yours in a way surface level work never can be. It remembers things about you not even your conscious mind can remember.

Together these two things produce art that looks absurd to anyone who isn't you. You've had dreams you couldn't explain. Imagine someone else trying to make sense of them. That confusion is actually the point; it creates a gravitational pull toward the person who made it. You want to know “why?” or “how?” and  they hold the power to explain.

## The Artist Is Now The Art

The Artist is Now the Art.

When people create intimate art, people don't just consume it. They seek the person behind it.

Basquiat is the clearest proof. People weren't buying his work because of technical skill, objectively it wasn't his strongest suit. They were buying into his story, his process, his specific and chaotic way of moving through the world. The intimacy of how he lived is what the art carried. You weren't just getting a painting. You were getting a fragment of a person.

Lucki, my favourite musician, does the same thing in a completely different form. He's a rapper known for how honest he is in his lyrics. In his earlier work especially he doesn't glorify anything. He just tells you the intimate parts of how he's been living and how he feels about it, not shying away from expressing the darkness of his life. His music is his diary made public.

That's the frame worth holding onto. What object carries the most concentrated version of a person's soul? Their diary. That's what art needs to function as going forward. Especially as soulless AI and algorthimic content floods everything else.

If someone read your diary they might mock you, yes. Vulnerability doesn't guarantee reward. But it can do something else. It creates obsession. People feel like they know you. They feel like they have a relationship with you. Every new piece of work becomes an update from someone they care about. Absence creates hunger. It's like your closest friend going quiet.

Art becomes relational here. You are the product. Not the paintings, the music, the literature. You. People are buying into feeling connected to a specific human being.

Virgil said something of the sort. You can design the candle or the room the candle is in. Your intimate relationship with yourself is now the room. That’s what sells everything inside of it.

## Transparency

If we are now flooded with soulless art made by machines, what is the currency human artists have left? Transparency. Owning everything publicly; beliefs, contradictions, spiritual stances, failures. The more legible you are as a full human being, the more irreplaceable your work becomes.

Could we replace FKA Twigs? No. She’s too much of herself in her art. Remove her from it and you lose the whole thing. I’m not saying everyone needs to overshare. I’m saying performance is collapsing. People are fatigued from performing. People are fatigued from knowingly watching others perform. We are all waiting for someone to be vulnerable enough to step outside of it and go first.

So if you’re really that passionate about what you do, why not go first?

I opened this essay saying the Wild West was a chaotic, unpredictable, a high risk and high reward era. Right now is honestly no different. Our gold rush isn’t gold. It’s intimacy.

The reward? I don’t know. Maybe your ambitions come true. Maybe you find something stable inside the uncertainty. The risk though is identity. If you put yourself out there intimately, know you’ve got nowhere to hide. You are emotionally naked.

It’s all a leap of faith.
