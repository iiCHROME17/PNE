# You Believe In A God, You Just Don't Like The Name

## The Refused

I’m addressing the concept of God here.

Not to argue you should believe in the Christian God, the Islamic God, or Buddha. I don’t care to say who’s right or wrong. This is not about how God exists in a particular tradition. This is much simpler and more universal.

Most people already believe in some higher source. An organising principle.

They just refuse to call it God.

People may refuse God in name, but function with faith that something greater than themselves is at work. Without evidence, they trust timing and believe things happen for a reason. They hope possibility may favour them.

To me, at the very least, God is the unknown.

What we call God is what we don’t know. We change what we call God as we learn, because our capacity to know evolves. But no matter how much we learn, we will never know it all. We maintain a relationship to the concept of the unknown, regardless of whether we know it as God or the Universe.

We all live ambiguous lives. To not be nihilistic, you must have faith in something you don’t know. That is where I think you believe in a God.

You just don’t like the name.

## You Don’t Know You Believe

When people say “I don’t believe in God,” I interpret this as: “I don’t believe in your version of God.” It’s less a rejection of the concept and more a rejection of human definitions. The rules. The institutions. The contradictions. The way God has been used to control, exclude, or shame. That refusal is reasonable. Human definitions of God are often more about human needs than spiritual truth.

But refusing the name doesn’t mean refusing the concept. You might call it the divine source, the universe, the unknown, or Mother Earth. The label doesn’t matter. What matters is whether you acknowledge the gap between what you can control and what actually shapes your experience. That gap, that unquantifiable influence, is where God operates.

Consider the ambitious person. In measurable reality, their success or innovation often seems impossible. Yet it manifests anyway. That “delusion” is often a relationship with unknown possibility, faith that unseen factors might favour them. There are forces beyond our perspective that we can only entrust to something greater, whether we call it God, luck, or timing.

That quiet trust is where the divine reveals itself.

Refusing to call it God doesn’t make it disappear. It just means your concept of a higher power goes unnamed or differently named.

What most people miss is that refusal can be a form of openness. When you say “I don’t believe in God,” you might quietly mean “I refuse rigid definitions of the divine.” That is not closed-mindedness. That is unannounced epistemic humility.

That is saying “I don’t know,” which is, paradoxically, the most spiritually honest position you can take.

## The Good Faith Imagination

It’s our not knowing which allows us to grow.

As long as, and as soon as, you can comfortably say “I know I don’t know,” you open yourself to influence and create space for imagination to operate. You can say “I imagine this is possible for me, so I’m going to try and make it real” without needing solid evidence to defend that imagination. Furthermore, it is the humility to understand that what exists in your imagination may materialise differently into the world than you expect.

This is important. The outcome rarely matches the exact vision. But that divergence isn’t failure. It’s feedback. It’s the divine revealing what you couldn’t see from where you started.

How it becomes real, even I don’t know. That’s where the divine operates. It is the gap between one’s intention and the outcome where God, the Universe, and the Unknown work in ways you cannot predict or control.

This, I call Good Faith Imagination. It is not blind optimism. It’s not manifestation without action either. It’s the childlike willingness to imagine a future you cannot see a path towards, paired with the humility to admit you do not control how the path shapes. Something greater than you will.

The “good faith” part matters. It means you act as if the unknown might favour you, without demanding proof it will. You don’t wait for certainty. You move with trust.

You imagine. You act. You trust something will reveal the way forwards.

## We Need Structure

You must perceive a possibility before you can pursue it. If you can’t imagine success, you won’t see the opportunities that lead to it. You’ll filter them out of your own reality. Your brain will categorise them as irrelevant noise because they don’t fit your prediction model of what’s possible for you.

But perception alone isn’t enough. Possibility only becomes reality through effort. Through iteration. Through the willingness to be wrong, adjust, and try again.

This is why “I don’t know” is so powerful. It keeps you receptive to new information. It allows you to update your approach when reality provides feedback. The person who says “I know exactly how this will work” has locked themselves into a single path. When it doesn’t work, they interpret that as personal failure rather than necessary redirection.

## The Wish For Better

If you wish for better and you don’t know the path forward, you already fit the criteria for believing in some God. You’re trusting that unseen factors might align in your favour. You’re hoping timing works out. You’re acting as if there’s more at work than just what you can control.

You just need to cultivate the relationship with it. Not through ritual or doctrine, necessarily. But through practice. Through the repeated choice to say “I don’t know how, but I’ll try.” Through staying open when outcomes diverge from expectations. Through trusting the gap between intention and result.

In doing so, you can move towards the imagined better. Not because you’ve figured it all out. But because you’ve positioned yourself to receive what emerges.
