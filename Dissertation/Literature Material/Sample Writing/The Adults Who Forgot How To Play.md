# The Adults Who Forgot How To Play

## Adult Life

I think one of the biggest problems people are facing approaching adult life is anxiety and isolation. People are scared, and it makes sense. Adult life is ambiguous. What’s worse; it’s lonely.

Community becomes scarce the older you get. This is the root issue. Without community, people lose two critical things.

First, feedback. When you’re navigating life without peers to reflect alongside you, how do you know what you’re doing is right? Who do you go to for advice? For new perspective? Who helps you answer the small uncertainties?

Am I falling behind? Is this normal? Should I be worried? Am I right to feel this way?

All of these start as questions and balloon into existential dread and self-hatred because there’s nobody to calibrate against.

Second, co-regulation. Human beings are social creatures. We are wired to create and experience with others. When you remove yourself from those two things, you will inevitably feel dysregulated. It’s community that is supposed to catch you when your nervous system spins out.

This spike in anxiety we’re experiencing isn’t mysterious. It’s isolation plus missing feedback loops . Without relational anchoring, everything feels more uncertain than it actually is.

But what if there’s another way?

## The Answer We Forgot

The solution seemingly exists already. Yet it has degraded itself from a solution into its own problem.

Community wasn’t something you had to build or beg for. It was ambient . You saw the same people at work, in your neighbourhood, in all these third spaces that once existed. You didn’t have to curate connection because it was already there.

You didn’t have to wonder if you were falling behind because you could see how everyone else was doing. Not their highlight reel, but their lived realities . What created solidarity then was the fact you saw people struggle and recover. You could relate to an experience instead of idealise it.

People were arguably more attuned to seeing your humanity than your role, given continuity. People could pick up on y

our changes. Your evolution and failings both. People had the capacity to see you for who you are.

This was never utopia. It didn’t function as perfectly as it could. People had capacity to see humanity in others, but did they actually value it?

You can’t romanticise the past. There were conditions to community in previous generations: conformity, surveillance, social pressure. But there were still accessible frameworks for relational anchoring. You constantly had access to a collective reality .

Then the collective reality collapsed.

Community didn’t become less valuable intrinsically. It became less coherent as knowledge and technology expanded. Work became remote and productivity got strictly measured. Churches got exposed for their over-reliance on mysticism and performative virtues.

Suddenly, these communities went from shared reality to either redundant or shared fantasy .

It seems individualism is growing while incentive for community is declining. Self-sufficiency becomes the value. Self-regulation becomes the standard. Eventually, vulnerability becomes a metric of shame instead of a natural attribute of one’s humanity.

And it gets reinforced every single day.

## The Social Media That Is

Social media emerged as a natural solution to declining community. Its original promises were exploration, expression, and connection. You could find people who thought like you, share ideas, and experiment with identity in ways physical communities often resisted.

Early online spaces were relational laboratories. Imperfect and ethically flawed, they nonetheless sometimes provided co-regulation and genuine communal engagement.

Over time, exploration calcified into performance . Platforms evolved from relational spaces into marketplaces where each person becomes a brand to be sold. Interactions became largely curated: highlight reels, filtered personalities, and quantifiable popularity dominate the landscape.

Connection exists, but only in theory. You can like, share, or comment, but it all becomes hollow very fast. Engagement is symbolic, no longer relational.

People end up needing more likes, more followers, more comments because it’s a poor substitute for the relational hunger we all desire. The mechanism that once gave us the anchoring we want has now been co-opted by systems that kill understanding in favour of symbolic engagement.

The result is social media now delivers self-regulation under surveillance , becoming a drug in itself. You monitor your image, measure your value, and hold yourself to a curated standard.

You crop yourself to become worthy of community, inviting instability and emotional dysregulation to achieve something that was supposed to promise stability.

Social media, as it is now, functions as a drug: a revolving door that offers only the illusion of community while demanding tolerance for the very anxieties real community is meant to alleviate.

## The Social Media That Was

Back in the early internet, identity was messy, flexible, and public in a way that felt normal. The “swag era,” the golden age of YouTube, and early blogs made it okay to try new things.

You could start a channel in your twenties without it feeling like some huge career move. Cringe wasn’t a big deal. It was part of playing around. Being vulnerable online wasn’t risky; it was just part of figuring things out.

Shame around what you posted was lower. Making mistakes, being awkward, or posting something silly didn’t carry the weight it does today. People could show themselves growing and changing, not just their successes. Experimenting, trying weird things, failing publicly, those things were normal, even fun. That made growing up online in the 2010s feel more social, more playful, and more human.

Now it’s different. Online identity is more about defense, exclusivity, and staying in place. Niche communities often gatekeep instead of letting people explore. Vulnerability feels risky, and self-expression gets judged fast. The freedom to mess up and experiment has mostly disappeared, replaced by curated images and careful personas.

Even our humour has changed. Most online jokes today are heavily post-ironic or reactive, often built around someone else’s social failings. Compare that to Vine and other early platforms a decade ago, where humour was expressive, immediate, and playful.

Basically, the internet that shaped older generations let people grow in public. Today’s platforms train people to defend, perform, and protect themselves. Identity has shifted from open play to cautious management, and with it, a lot of the natural connection that used to happen online has faded.

Social media now actively discourages self-exploration. To me, it’s almost an enemy in some ways. It demands continuity over experimentation. And somehow, even with more knowledge and access than ever before, many of us are discovering ourselves slower.

Gen Z are the adults that forgot how to play.

## The Economy Of Chalance

Adulthood today is defined by nonchalance. It’s incentivised to be measured, distant, and ambiguous. Detachment is both expected and overabundant. Everywhere we look, people signal control, composure, and curated perfection.

But in a world where everyone is doing one thing, attention stops responding to that thing . That’s the law of the attention economy.

The result is paradoxical: the very behaviours we once socially rewarded become invisible. We seem to always want what is unavailable. So if polish is all that’s available, eventually we will want mess .

The social attention economy suggests this shift is already happening beyond our awareness. What we all might be dying for is presence and permission to be messy . That is why I call it the Economy of Chalance .

What’s becoming novel isn’t detachment from life.It’s attachment to it .

Being attached, being passionate, being visibly human are now the scarcest signals. Scarcity is the engine of attention .

This is why we see a turn towards nostalgic aesthetics, physical media, non-digital experience. These things resonate because they’re flawed . They aren’t constructed for symbolic, algorithmic engagement. They’re constructed for genuine resonance.

Detachment and attachment on a cultural scale appear to be a cycle, like yin and yang. It’s only natural we progress from one and return to the other. Novel value may exist in choosing not to perform and engaging with the mess that makes one human.

The internet might need to return to a place much like that of iCarly , where being online was more about exploration, experimentation, and self-discovery. Not performance or curation. Just public mess .

Performance has reached its upper limit. What must come next isn’t more polish. It’s permission to be unfinished .
