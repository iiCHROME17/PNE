# Chapter 1: Introduction

## 1.1 Introduction

Dialogue systems in video games have historically been implemented through manually authored branching conversation trees, in which every possible exchange is predetermined and scripted by a developer. Commercially deployed examples — such as the conversation systems found in *Mass Effect* (BioWare, 2007) and *The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt* (CD Projekt Red, 2015) — demonstrate both the expressive potential and the fundamental limitations of this paradigm. Whilst such systems can deliver rich, authored narrative experiences, they do so at considerable authorial cost: the addition of each new non-player character (NPC) or branching pathway demands a proportionate investment in writing, voice performance, and quality assurance. More critically, scripted dialogue trees are psychologically shallow. NPCs in these systems do not reason; they match conditions to pre-written responses. They possess no internal beliefs, no emergent goals, and no capacity for genuine adaptive behaviour in response to the conversational dynamics of a specific interaction.

The limitations of script-driven NPC dialogue have been well-documented within the interactive narrative research community. Mateas and Stern (2003), in their foundational work on *Façade*, demonstrated that believable character behaviour could be generated from a drama-managed system in which NPCs maintained internal emotional and relational state — a departure from the dialogue-tree model. Young et al. (2013) further established that narrative generation as a planning problem, in which story events are produced by intelligent agents pursuing goals, represents a more principled approach to interactive storytelling. In parallel, research into the Belief-Desire-Intention (BDI) model of rational agency — introduced by Bratman (1987) and formalised as a computational architecture by Rao and Georgeff (1995) — has provided a cognitive framework that maps naturally onto the design of believable agents: characters that hold beliefs about the world, form desires in response to those beliefs, and commit to intentions that guide their behaviour.

Recent developments in locally-deployable large language models (LLMs), such as those made available through the Ollama runtime, have introduced a new practical dimension to this research space. For the first time, conversational text generation of sufficient quality for game dialogue can be performed on consumer hardware without dependence on cloud-hosted inference services. This creates an opportunity to combine principled cognitive modelling with high-quality surface-level text generation — separating the *reasoning* about what an NPC should say from the *production* of the words themselves.

The Psychological Narrative Engine (PNE), the system documented in this dissertation, was developed in response to these opportunities. The PNE is a middleware system, exposed as a REST and WebSocket API, that drives dynamic NPC conversations within game engines such as Unity, Godot, and Unreal Engine. Rather than relying on authored dialogue trees, the PNE models each NPC through a three-layer personality representation — cognitive, social, and world — and processes each player turn through a staged BDI pipeline: from cognitive interpretation of the player's input, through desire formation and social intention selection, to outcome application and FSM-based narrative routing. Dialogue text is generated by a locally-running LLM, constrained by a closed vocabulary of nineteen canonical behavioural intentions to prevent unconstrained hallucination.

The principal contributions of this work are as follows:

- A modular BDI processing pipeline in which the cognitive reasoning stage operates without LLM involvement, drawing instead from a library of 810 template-matched thought patterns to produce predictable, author-controllable NPC cognition.
- A two-dice skill-check mechanism, inspired by the approach taken in *Disco Elysium* (ZA/UM, 2019), in which the probability of a conversational success is computed from player skill attributes and NPC personality parameters, displayed to the player prior to commitment, with a recovery mechanic that prevents narrative coherence breakdown upon failure.
- An API-first architecture that decouples the dialogue engine from any specific game engine, enabling integration across multiple target platforms through a common HTTP and WebSocket interface.
- A judgement-score-driven finite state machine (FSM) that aggregates conversational outcomes across turns into a single 0–100 scalar, providing a stable, predictable signal for narrative branching without dependence on LLM output classification.

This dissertation does not present findings from a formal user study; evaluation is grounded in structured conversation logs and analysis of the system's internal state across representative test scenarios. Multiplayer dialogue, persistent world state across sessions, and procedural scenario generation are outside the scope of this project.

## 1.2 Project Overview

### 1.2.1 Objectives

The Psychological Narrative Engine was developed with three overarching objectives. The first was to design and prototype a dialogue simulation system in which NPC behaviour is grounded in cognitive psychology, such that NPC responses emerge from a representation of internal mental state rather than from pre-authored scripts. The second was to demonstrate that systems-thinking principles — specifically the use of layered feedback loops and multi-level modelling — can be applied computationally to produce emergent narrative behaviour within an interactive game context. The third was to produce a documented, game-engine-ready API suitable for integration into externally developed interactive environments.

### 1.2.2 System Architecture

At a high level, each conversational turn in the PNE is processed through the following pipeline. A player selects a dialogue choice — represented as a JSON object encoding the spoken text, tone weights across four social skill dimensions (authority, diplomacy, empathy, manipulation), and any prerequisite skill or relationship requirements. The choice is first evaluated against hard gates that may exclude it from availability based on the player's current skill ratings or relational standing with the NPC. If available, a weighted dice roll is performed to determine whether the choice succeeds, with success probability derived from the player's skill attributes, the NPC's personality parameters, the current player–NPC relationship, and a configured difficulty level. The BDI pipeline then processes the outcome: the NPC's cognitive layer generates an internal thought response and subjective interpretation; the desire layer extracts a goal-oriented state from that thought; the social layer selects a behavioural intention from the canonical intention registry; and the outcome layer applies state changes — adjustments to the NPC's stance, relational disposition, and the player's accumulated judgement score. The LLM then generates the NPC's spoken response, guided by a structured prompt encoding the NPC's identity, BDI state, emotional expression parameters, and a node-specific scene direction. Finally, the FSM evaluates the updated judgement score and intention against the transition conditions defined in the scenario graph, routing the conversation to the appropriate next node or terminal state.

The system is deployed as a FastAPI server. Game engine clients communicate with the engine via REST endpoints (for session creation, choice retrieval, and state persistence) and via WebSocket connections for token-by-token streaming of NPC dialogue.

### 1.2.3 Scope and Constraints

The PNE is scoped to single-NPC, turn-based conversational interactions. NPC personalities and conversation scenarios are defined in JSON configuration files authored prior to runtime. The system is dependent on a locally-running Ollama instance for dialogue generation; an offline mode is provided for testing purposes in environments where LLM inference is unavailable. Real-time multiplayer dialogue, persistent narrative state across independent play sessions, and procedural generation of NPC personalities or scenario graphs are outside the scope of this project.

### 1.2.4 Dissertation Structure

The remainder of this dissertation is structured as follows. Chapter 2 presents a review of the relevant literature, covering BDI agent architectures, prior work in interactive narrative and NPC dialogue systems, and the role of large language models in conversational AI. Chapter 3 documents the requirements analysis and system design. Chapter 4 describes the implementation of the PNE, examining each pipeline component in detail. Chapter 5 evaluates the system against the stated objectives using structured conversation logs and state analysis. Chapter 6 concludes with a reflection on the project's contributions, limitations, and directions for future work.
